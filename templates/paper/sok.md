# Systematization (SoK) paper — section skeleton

> Loaded by /research.paper when proposal.md paper type is systematization (SoK).

Use this skeleton when the paper's core question is "what does the field actually know about X, organized so the community can build on it?". The proof obligation is **two things, not one**: a NOVEL, defensible taxonomy/framework, PLUS lessons/insights that appear in *no single* prior paper. An SoK is not a new system, and it is not a literature dump - it is reorganization that produces new understanding.

This is a section menu, not a fixed mold. Reorder, merge, or drop beats with a one-line reason; the taxonomy and the lessons are the only non-negotiables.

Replace every bracketed placeholder.

---

## Abstract

One paragraph: **the mess → the organizing contribution → the payoff**.

- Name the confusion: fragmented terminology, incomparable results, or a field that has grown faster than its map.
- State the taxonomy/framework as the contribution, and the one or two lessons it surfaces that prior work missed.
- Close with the agenda you hand the community, not "more research is needed."

## 1. Introduction

[Why systematize, and why now: the area is large enough to have lessons but tangled enough that nobody can see them. Show the mess concretely - conflicting definitions, apples-to-oranges comparisons, repeated rediscovery. State precisely what you contribute: a taxonomy, an analysis through it, and a research agenda. Preview one non-obvious lesson. End with a contribution list and a roadmap.]

## 2. Scope & methodology

[Where credibility is earned. Define inclusion/exclusion criteria so a reader could reproduce your corpus: venues, date range, search terms, snowballing, and how borderline papers were judged. Report counts (papers screened -> papers included). Honest scope bounds - what you deliberately leave out - prevent the "you missed X" review.]

## 3. Taxonomy / framework

[The organizing contribution. Present the axes/dimensions and argue *why these axes* - what question each one answers and what distinction it makes visible. Aim for MECE-ish: categories that are clear, near-non-overlapping, and jointly cover the space. Show the taxonomy as a figure or table, and place one or two known works to demonstrate it discriminates.]

## 4. Systematization body

[Apply the taxonomy to the corpus. Go per dimension: what the field has tried, where approaches cluster, what is well-trodden vs. empty. Then the cross-cutting observations - patterns visible only once the work is laid on this frame (recurring assumptions, blind spots, contradictory results). A populated taxonomy table doubles as a map of the field.]

## 5. Lessons learned / open problems / research agenda

[The payoff, and the part reviewers remember. Each lesson must be earned by the systematization - synthesized across papers, not quotable from any one of them. Distinguish settled lessons, open problems, and false leads. Convert open problems into a concrete, prioritized agenda the community can act on.]

## 6. Related surveys & SoKs

[Position against prior surveys and SoKs on or near this topic, not just the primary literature. For each, state in one line how yours differs and why a new systematization was needed: newer scope, a sharper organizing axis, or lessons the earlier survey could not yet see.]

## 7. Conclusion

[Under one column. Restate the organizing insight and the single most important lesson, point to the agenda, and stop. Do not rehash the taxonomy, list sections, or add new claims.]

---

## Quality checklist

- [ ] The taxonomy/framework is novel - not lifted from a prior survey - and is MECE-ish (clear, near-non-overlapping, jointly exhaustive).
- [ ] Selection methodology is reproducible: criteria, sources, date range, and counts are stated.
- [ ] Each lesson is synthesized across the corpus and findable in *no single* cited paper.
- [ ] Open problems are turned into a concrete, prioritized research agenda.
- [ ] Positioned explicitly against prior surveys/SoKs, with a one-line delta each.
- [ ] No new-system claim is smuggled in: the contribution is understanding, not artifact.
- [ ] Abstract leads with the mess → organizing contribution → payoff.
